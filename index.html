the places i have lived.

This is a short chronicle of (most of) the places I've lived. Each city is accompanied by a song; the songs are not necessarily representative of the cities, but rather songs that make me think of the cities for personal reasons (or that were very popular while I lived there).

  • Andrews, TX
  • Austin, TX
  • Kharkiv, Ukraine
  • Moscow, Russia
  • Albuquerque, NM
  • Tartu, Estonia
  • Philadelphia, PA

Andrews, TX 1992 – 2010

A banner seen at the city line. © Julie Dermansky, DeSmog

Andrews is a small, remote town on the plains near the border with New Mexico. Friday night football, oil, and church are pretty much the name of the game. It is hundreds of miles away from the nearest city of any sort of national significance, but the region as a whole punches above its weight economically because of the scale of oil and gas production that goes on there. It's a city that never sleeps, with the drone of semi trucks and the low, steady clang of oil pumpjacks filling the air all through the night.

Austin, TX 2010 – 2013

Paddleboating on Lake Travis, Austin. (Source: Unsplash)

I went to college at the University of Texas at Austin. Moving to Austin was a bit of culture shock for me because I hadn't really spent any time in cities. Because of its politics, music scene, and hippie-friendly vibes, it is something of an island deep in the heart of Texas – which is encapsulated by the city's unofficial motto, "Keep Austin Weird." Life in Austin is defined by barbecue, Texas country, tacos for breakfast, tubing down the river, traffic, heat, eight-o-clocks standing on the Congress Avenue Bridge watching bats fill the sky, etc.

Kharkiv, Ukraine 2013 – 2014

"Historical Museum" metro station in central Kharkiv. (Source: Unsplash)

Kharkiv is the largest city in the eastern part of Ukraine, and I spent my final year of college studying abroad there. Home to many of the country's top-ranking universities, it is a largely Russian-speaking city but is nonetheless an important center of Ukrainian cultural life. My program there was cut short by the turmoil caused by Russia's invasion of Crimea in 2014. Now Russia has set its sights on Kharkiv as essentially ground zero of its crusade to wipe Ukraine off the map.

Moscow, Russia 2011, 2012, 2014 – 2015

Exhibition of the Achievements of the National Economy park ("VDNKh"). (Source: Unsplash)

Russia – with all its many flaws – holds a special place in my heart. I spent three different stints there studying Russian (in summer 2011 and summer v2012) and interning at a university (2014-15). Moscow is one of the world's largest cities, and it is a city of lots of contrasts. It is breathtakingly historical and grotesquely modern; it is oppressively concrete, but also boasts some of the world's nicest urban green spaces; it's home to the most generous and most repugnant people on the planet. While you're unlikely to be visiting Moscow or Russia any time soon, it definitely deserves a spot on your bucket list - even if it comes dead last.

Albuquerque, New Mexico 2015 – 2016

A wealthy foothills neighborhood in Albuquerque. (Source: Unsplash)

Albuquerque is New Mexico's largest city, an isolated desert landscape at the foothills of the Sandia Mountains. Characterized by green chiles, stucco houses, wind-swept mesas, Navajo fry bread and Breaking Bad, the pace of life there is notoriously slow. There's not really too much going on in Albuquerque, but the gorgeous sunsets, amazing food, and easy proximity to mountains, forests, rivers, and ancient native settlements make it an attractive place to live. But Albuquerque is no Seattle – New Mexico is one of the country's poorest states and issues such as violent crime, drug addiction, and childhood poverty rear their head in an unfortunate way in the city.

Tartu, Estonia 2016 – 2018

Tartu Town Hall Square (Raekoja plats). (Source: Unsplash)

I lived in Estonia for two years during grad school. Estonia is a tiny country of around 1.3 million people wedged in the corner of northeastern Europe. Culturally, it has heavy German and Nordic influences, and the national language is a close sibling of Finnish. However, it also has a large Russian-speaking population – it was part of the Russian Empire for 200 years and then following WWII, it was occupied by the Soviet Union. Today, Estonia is a very peaceful, quiet country with a booming tech and tourism industry and endless forests, bogs, and lakes. But if you go during the winter be ready to suffer, because the sun sets in November and doesn't come back out until April.

Philadelphia, Pennsylvania 2018 – Present

Fairmount Water Works and Philadelphia Art Museum. (Source: Unsplash)

Ah, the city of brotherly love. I moved here for work in 2018. I have a love-hate relationship with Philadelphia, but doesn't everyone?
